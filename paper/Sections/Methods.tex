\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

We use two instruments: an agent-based simulator in which the paper's assumptions hold by
construction (RQ1 and supporting studies), and a counterfactual replay of real Polymarket
price paths (RQ2). Both live in a single Python package, \texttt{srpm}; the full API is in
the repository.

\paragraph{Simulator.}
The simulator instantiates the model of Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} directly. A
\texttt{SignalStructure} is a conditional table $\mathbb{P}(X\mid Y)$; we use
\texttt{binary\_symmetric}$(q)$ (correct with probability $q$) and
\texttt{ground\_truth}$(\rho)$ (a near-oracle). Because signals are conditionally
independent given $Y$ (A4), posterior log-odds are \emph{additive}: a truthful Bayesian
inverts the previous report and adds its own signal's log-likelihood ratio $\ell$, the
computational core that makes exact aggregation possible. The deviation family is
\texttt{ScaledSignal}$(\gamma)$, which reports $\mathrm{logit}(\pi)+\gamma\,\ell$, so
$\gamma=1$ is truthful and $\gamma>1$ over-confident. \texttt{CrossEntropyMSR} implements
\eqref{eq:cemsr}, and \texttt{SelfResolvingMarket} runs agents, terminates with probability
$\alpha$, designates the terminal agent as reference, and applies the CE-MSR and flat fee.
By construction this satisfies A1--A4; the incentive study (RQ1) then varies only A5, while
the stress tests below deliberately break A1 and A2 one at a time, making the simulator a
clean test of both the mechanism's \emph{core} incentive claim and its robustness to its own
modelling assumptions.

\paragraph{RQ1: profit-maximising deviation.}
We isolate a focal agent (signal quality $q_t=0.7$) and let it consider $\gamma\in[0,3]$
($61$ points). The reference observes the focal report \emph{plus} $k$ independent signals
of quality $q_r=0.65$ (its informational substitutes) for
$k\in\{0,2,8,32,64,128,256\}$. For each $(k,\gamma)$ we estimate the focal agent's expected
CE-MSR payoff by Monte Carlo ($N=4\times10^5$, prior $\pi=0.5$) and report
$\gamma^\star(k)=\arg\max_\gamma$ payoff. This operationalises Theorem~3: $\gamma^\star$
should fall from ``over-report'' toward the truthful value $1$ as $k$ grows.

\paragraph{Analytic $k_{\min}$.}
To connect to the closed-form theory we reproduce the paper's minimum-substitutes bound
(their Figure~2): the smallest $k$ such that the deviation gain under the CE-MSR is at most
$\varepsilon$. Chaining Theorem~1 (bounds $|\Delta|$ in $k$), Theorem~5 (bounds the gain
$\hat D_\eta(\Delta,y)$ in $\Delta$), and Remark~3 (numerically solve
$\varepsilon' = \min\{|\Delta| : \hat D_\eta(\Delta,y)=\varepsilon\}$, in a log-transformed
variable so roots near the pole are resolved) gives
$k_{\min}(\delta,\eta,\varepsilon,y_1) = \frac{1}{-\log(1-\delta)}\log\frac{(1-\eta)/\eta - \eta/(1-\eta)}{4\varepsilon'}$,
which we evaluate across the market prior $y_1$.

\paragraph{Supporting studies.}
Three further studies probe aggregation rather than incentives: \emph{(i) aggregation},
sweep the number of truthful agents and the signal quality, measuring the gap between the
terminal report and the full-information Bayesian posterior; \emph{(ii) informed reference},
vary the fraction of near-oracle agents (reliability $0.99$) in a $12$-agent market;
\emph{(iii) mixed populations}, compare an all-truthful population against ones seeded
with herders, noisy reporters, an uninformative third, and over-reporters, measuring
accuracy, gap to full information, and the designer's total cost.

\paragraph{Breaking one assumption at a time (A1, A2).}
We complement RQ1 (which varies A5) by breaking two further assumptions in the same clean
world and measuring how far the terminal reference drifts from the ideal full-information
posterior (an aggregation measure, not payout agreement). For \textbf{A1} we perturb its
\emph{Bayesian-rationality component} (not risk neutrality): every agent adds zero-mean
Gaussian noise of scale $\sigma$ (in log-odds) to its truthful report, so $\sigma=0$ is the
exact-Bayesian baseline. For \textbf{A2}, the world draws $Y$ under the true prior but the
market shares a single \emph{mis-specified} common prior a logit gap $d$ from it, so $d=0$ is
the correct-prior baseline; the agents still agree with one another, they are just
collectively wrong. Both sweeps use $N=8$ agents of signal quality $q=0.6$, prior $\pi=0.5$,
and $8000$ Monte Carlo runs per point, with independent RNG streams (spawned from one seed)
so the two are reproducible and decoupled. Each knob recovers the assumption at its baseline,
where the gap is $0$ by construction.

\paragraph{RQ2: Polymarket counterfactual.}
We download roughly $90$ \emph{resolved} Polymarket markets~\parencite{polymarket} with
volume above \$100k (responses cached). Each Yes-price $p_t$ is a report
$\mathbf{q}^{(t)}=[\,1-p_t,\,p_t\,]$ and each price \emph{change} is one ``reporter''. We
pay every transition two ways on the identical set of transitions: \emph{self-resolving}
(CE-MSR \eqref{eq:cemsr} against a late reference price $\mathbf{r}$, no outcome) and
\emph{verifiable} (log market scoring rule against the realised $Y$). A late reference is
both better-informed \emph{and} contaminated by outcome leakage, so we sweep how late
$\mathbf{r}$ is: \texttt{lead\_1h/24h/168h} before the last point, \texttt{life\_50pct}
(halfway through the market's life), \texttt{window\_1-24h} (an averaged late window);
and additionally sweep by \emph{relative} time to close. For each market and reference we
compute the Pearson and Spearman correlation of per-reporter payouts (do the \emph{amounts},
and the \emph{ranking} of earners, agree?), the top-decile overlap, and the ratio of total
payout. \emph{This measures whether the mechanism's \textbf{outputs} track the verifiable
mechanism's, not incentive compatibility: we replay fixed paths and never let agents
deviate, and real prices violate A1--A5. RQ2 is thus a robustness test, complementary to
RQ1.}
