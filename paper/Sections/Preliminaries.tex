\section{Preliminaries: Model, Mechanism, and Assumptions}
\label{sec:preliminaries}

We summarise the model of \textcite{srinivasan2025selfresolving}; see the original for
proofs.

\paragraph{Setting.}
The goal is to elicit and aggregate probabilistic predictions about a binary outcome
$Y\in\{0,1\}$ (categorical and discretised-continuous outcomes reduce to this case). $M$
agents arrive one at a time. Agent $t$ has a private signal $X^{(t)}$ from a common prior
and, on arrival, observes all previous reports $\mathbf{q}^{(1:t-1)}$; it forms a posterior
$\bar{\mathbf p}^{(t)} = \mathbb{P}(Y \mid X^{(t)}, \mathbf{q}^{(1:t-1)})$ and reports some
$\mathbf{q}^{(t)} \in \Delta\Omega_Y$. A truthful agent reports its posterior. We write $r$
for the reference agent and $y_1 = \mathbb{P}(Y{=}1)$.

\paragraph{From outcome to reference.}
A proper scoring rule $S(i,\mathbf{q})$ rewards a report against a realised outcome $i$ and
is maximised by honesty~\parencite{gneiting2007strictly}; the canonical choice is the log
rule $S(i,\mathbf{q}) = \log q_i$. The key move is to score a report against another
\emph{prediction} $\mathbf{r}$ rather than an outcome, via the cross-entropy rule
$S_{CE}(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{q}) = -H(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{q}) = \sum_i r_i \log q_i$, whose
expectation is maximised by reporting one's expectation of $\mathbf{r}$. The mechanism uses
its sequential, shared form, the \emph{cross-entropy market scoring rule} (CE-MSR),
\begin{equation}
    S_{CEM}(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{q}^{(t)}, \mathbf{q}^{(t-1)})
    = \sum_i r_i \log\!\left(\frac{q^{(t)}_i}{q^{(t-1)}_i}\right),
    \label{eq:cemsr}
\end{equation}
which pays an agent for how much closer to the reference its report is than the previous
one. Swapping the outcome $i$ for a reference $\mathbf{r}$ is what lets the market resolve
without an outcome.

\paragraph{The mechanism.}
Agents report in sequence; after each report the market terminates with probability
$\alpha$ (Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism}). The terminal agent $T$ (best-informed, having
seen the full history) is the reference. Agents $1,\dots,T-k$ are paid the CE-MSR
\eqref{eq:cemsr} against $\mathbf{q}^{(T)}$; the last $k$ agents (too close to the reference
to be scored against it) receive a flat fee $R$. The single parameter $k$ trades a
better-informed reference against paying more agents the flat fee.

\paragraph{Assumptions.}
The guarantees rest on five assumptions, and our studies are organised around when they
hold: \textbf{(A1)} agents are risk-neutral, fully rational Bayesians, common knowledge;
\textbf{(A2)} signals and outcome share a common prior; \textbf{(A3)} each signal induces a
\emph{unique} posterior, so reports can be inverted (distinct signals); \textbf{(A4)}
signals are conditionally independent given $Y$, a sufficient form of informational
substitutes~\parencite{chen2016informational}, the structure under which standard markets
reward prompt truthful revelation~\parencite{chen2010gaming}; \textbf{(A5)} signals are
$(\delta,\eta)$-informative: $\delta$ lower-bounds how much each signal separates $Y{=}0$
from $Y{=}1$ (via the Bhattacharyya coefficient $BC = 1-\delta$), and $\eta$ lower-bounds
signal likelihoods so no single report is arbitrarily decisive.

\paragraph{What the paper proves.}
The mechanism is paid by the cross-entropy \emph{market} scoring rule, whose guarantees
express one idea: \emph{a well-informed reference makes honesty optimal} (Theorems~2--3 are
the analogues for the non-market scoring rule).
\emph{Theorem 1:} a focal agent's influence on the reference posterior, the adjustment
$\Delta$, shrinks geometrically,
$|\Delta| \le \tfrac14(\tfrac{1-\eta}{\eta}-\tfrac{\eta}{1-\eta})(1-\delta)^k$.
\emph{Theorem 4:} paid by the CE-MSR against a truthful reference, agent $t$ strictly maximises
payoff by reporting truthfully once the reference has at least an explicit threshold number
of signals. \emph{Theorem 5:} the gain from any deviation is bounded by
$\hat D_\eta(\Delta,\mathbf{y})$, which $\to 0$ as $k$ grows (Theorem~1); and truthful
reporting is a strict perfect Bayesian equilibrium of the self-resolving market (Theorem~6).
Theorem~1 motivates the analytic $k_{\min}$ we reproduce; Theorem~5 is exactly what RQ1 tests.

\paragraph{Research questions.}
\textbf{RQ1 (simulation):} where A1--A4 hold by construction, when does a freely deviating
agent find truthful reporting a best response, as a function of the one assumption we vary
(A5, how informed the reference is)? \textbf{RQ2 (empirical):} on real markets, where
essentially none of A1--A5 hold, how closely do self-resolving payouts track the verifiable
(outcome-based) payouts they replace?
